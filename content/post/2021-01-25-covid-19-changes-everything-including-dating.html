Covid-19 has created probably the most severe disruption in history and it has affected everything we know, massively changing our lives. It is that topic that everyone is talking about for more than a year already, generating collective concerns and responsibility. Uncertainty is the new normal nowadays and we are all trying to cope with it.

Surprisingly or not, dating has also changed during the pandemic. The economic fallout, lockdowns and restrictions have affected not only our perceptions, but also our expectations and the way perceive people. For this purpose, Singles in America, a initiative powered by the Match Group, surveyed 5,000 americans back in August 2020 and revealed how people’s attitudes have evolved as a result of Covid-19.

Roughly one in three people purposely stopped dating when the pandemic started, typically deliverately for focusing on other areas. Unfortunately, one in five people found themselves in the position being dumped at the beginning of the pandemic. Even though the figures are quite low, it is enough to reveal an interesting pattern of reassessing priorities and focusing on the things that matter at the cost of postponing dating. Unfortunately, for some people that involved even breaking up with their current partner, if the things didn’t seem to move in the right direction.

Financial stability matters more during uncertain times

Investigating this topic further, people who took a break from dating during pandemic were asked what changed during their time off from dating. Top answers reveal that singles are more eager to find a financially stable partner, more likely to date someone who is employed full-time or more inclined to spend less money on dating. It looks like people have started to put some importance on finding a financiallly stable partner, hoping that it will eventually lead to emotional stability as well. Everyone agrees that financial uncertainties could shake a relationship, but being unemployed is really such a deal-breaker? Apparently, only one in eight people are more open to date someone who is unemployed now.

Love, delayed - We’re not ready to begin dating again and we’re cautious about it

Another topic worth considering is how ready people are to begin dating in-person again. Two thirds of people think it is either too soon or that it requires some precautions. Some people are excited and very ready to get back to their usual dates, but very few have already started or have never stoped dating. Since the pandemic hasn’t been officially ended, some people are still reluctant to shake hands, being difficult to trust anyone who’s willing to enter our lives. Does that also affect the way we approach dates? Researchers even found that those who felt more vulnerable to disease made them less interested in their prospective dates.

What’s changing?

People are being more selective and more intentional not only about whom they are reaching out to in the first place, but also about where we go on that first date. Parasitic organisms have affected human beings throughout history, but now we better understand how we can use our personal health, hygiene and social distance as barriers against those threats, but this is shaking the trust we put in other people. One in five persons will be more selective about who they hug, kiss or shake hands with, being slightly hesitant when it comes to trust someone else. Some people will insist on wearing masks throughout the date, while some others have become more drastic, insisting on eating outside at a restaurant or having only dates that involve outdoor activities.

The stakes are now higher.

During the pandemic, a majority of daters didn’t want to leave homes at all, which came as a huge impediment for dating other people. As a consequence, the dating apps have kept up the pace with the new requirements and included video functionalities, which gave birth to a new phenomenon: the video date. An extensive screening process has replaced the old-fashioned first dates and now more than ever people want to make sure the person they’re interested in is worth stepping out for.

Covid-19 has made dating harder than it was before. The only way to meet people during these times is online, using social media or online dating apps. But it can take weeks or months to build a meaningful relationship and it’s more labourious than ever. Sometimes even promising relationships fail because people aren’t at their best right now, being surrounded by uncertainty, disease and financial instability. But one of the things that this crisis may have taught us is that we need to be more honest with ourselves and have deeper, more meaningful conversations with the people we’re dating.

Sources: Singles in America, 2020; BBC, 2020; VOX, 2020.
